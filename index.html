The Problem

AI in law is inevitable.
The liability is optional.

Attorneys and paralegals are using AI tools every day — but most are doing it without guardrails. Pasting client documents into ChatGPT. Submitting AI-drafted briefs without disclosing it. Relying on citations that don't exist. One mistake can cost a career.

⚠️
Mata v. Avianca (S.D.N.Y. 2023) Attorneys sanctioned after submitting AI-generated briefs containing fabricated case citations. The court imposed $5,000 in sanctions and required submission of declarations from the attorneys involved. This is now the defining precedent on attorney AI accountability.

ABA Alignment

Every feature maps to a rule.
Not an accident.

ClearCounsel was designed around ABA Model Rules and formal ethics opinions — including Formal Opinion 477R on cybersecurity, Opinion 498 on virtual practice, and the 2024 guidance on generative AI. It's compliance infrastructure, not just a convenience tool.

Rule 1.1
Technological Competence Attorneys must understand and supervise AI tools used in their practice.
Rule 1.6
Confidentiality of Information Client data must be protected — including before it enters any third-party AI system.
Rule 3.3
Candor Toward the Tribunal AI use in court filings must be disclosed. Standing orders vary by court.
Opinion 477R
Cybersecurity Firms must vet third-party technology for data handling, security, and retention.
2024 AI Guidance
Generative AI in Practice Lawyers retain full responsibility for AI-generated work product submitted to courts.
